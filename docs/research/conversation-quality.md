# Conversation Quality & Depth — Scientific Research

## 1. Substantive Conversation and Happiness

### Mehl et al. — "Eavesdropping on Happiness" (2010)

- **Mehl, M.R., Vazire, S., Holleran, S.E., & Clark, C.S. (2010).** "Eavesdropping on Happiness: Well-Being Is Related to Having Less Small Talk and More Substantive Conversations." *Psychological Science*, 21(4), 539-541.

Mehl's team fitted 79 adults with an Electronically Activated Recorder (EAR) that periodically sampled ambient audio over 4 days, capturing naturalistic conversation. All conversation was coded as small talk or substantive.

**Key Findings:**
- The happiest participants had substantially *more* substantive conversations and *less* small talk
- Substantive conversation correlated positively with happiness (r = .36); small talk correlated negatively (r = −.36)
- Effects held after controlling for extraversion and other personality variables
- Both causal directions are plausible: happy people seek depth AND depth produces happiness

**Practical Implications:**
- Encourage quality over quantity — a few deep conversations matter more than many surface-level check-ins
- Post-contact notes capturing *what was actually discussed* signal depth was achieved
- Prompt deeper questions before contact to raise conversation quality

## 2. Conversations End Too Soon

### Mastroianni et al. — "Do Conversations End When People Want Them To?" (2021)

- **Mastroianni, A.M., Reece, A.G., Ravi, S., Cooney, G., Aknin, L.B., & Gilbert, D.T. (2021).** "Do Conversations End When People Want Them To?" *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 118(10), e2011809118.

A series of 10 preregistered studies involving thousands of participants measured when people *wanted* a conversation to end vs. when it actually did.

**Key Findings:**
- In 69–98% of cases, both parties wanted the conversation to continue longer than it did
- Both participants underestimated how much their partner wanted to keep talking
- The "premature ending" effect was robust across phone calls, in-person conversations, and conversations with strangers

**Why It Matters for a CRM:**
People nearly always appreciate more contact than they receive. The barrier is the initiator's miscalculation of the other person's desire, not actual disinterest. This directly parallels Liu et al. (2023) on reaching out — we consistently underestimate others' social appetite.

## 3. The Power of Follow-Up Questions

- **Huang, K., Yeomans, M., Brooks, A.W., Minson, J., & Gino, F. (2017).** "It Doesn't Hurt to Ask: Question-Asking Increases Liking." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 113(3), 430-452.

**Key Findings:**
- Asking more questions is reliably associated with being liked more
- **Follow-up questions** — referencing something the other person just said — are the most powerful type: they signal genuine listening, not just a question list
- The effect holds across text, chat, and in-person conversations

**What Makes Questions "Deep"?** Two components from self-disclosure research:
1. **Intimacy level** — questions that invite reflection on values, fears, hopes, identity (not just facts)
2. **Bidirectionality** — the asker also shares; reciprocal disclosure generates greater liking (Sprecher et al., 2013)

## 4. Active Listening as a Relationship Behavior

- **Weger, H., Bell, G.C., Minei, E., & Robinson, M. (2014).** "The Relative Effectiveness of Active Listening in Initial Interactions." *International Journal of Listening*, 28(1), 13-31.

Active listening (paraphrasing, clarifying questions, non-verbal engagement) produced significantly higher:
- Perceptions of feeling understood
- Relationship satisfaction
- Reported intimacy

vs. simple positive acknowledgment or advice-giving.

**The Core Shift:** Moving from *listening to respond* to *listening to understand*. People can detect this and respond powerfully to it.

---

## 5. Practical Implications for a Personal CRM

- **Pre-contact prompts** with suggested deep questions tailored to what's going on in that person's life
- **Post-contact notes** capturing what was actually discussed (not just "spoke to X")
- **Conversation depth signal** — was this a check-in or a real conversation?
- **Question banks** per relationship context (catching up after a long gap, checking in during a hard time, celebrating good news)

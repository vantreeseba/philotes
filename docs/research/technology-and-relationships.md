# Technology, Relationships & Intentional Design — Scientific Research

## 1. The Phone Presence Effect

- **Misra, S., Cheng, L., Genevie, J., & Yuan, M. (2016).** "The iPhone Effect: The Quality of In-Person Social Interactions in the Presence of Mobile Devices." *Environment and Behavior*, 48(2), 275-298.

Even when not actively used, phones on tables during conversations:
- Reduced conversation quality and depth
- Reduced empathic concern
- Reduced feelings of connectedness
- The effect was *stronger* for more personally meaningful topics

**Practical Implication:** A CRM could include a "be present" reminder before scheduled time with important people — a simple prompt to put the phone away.

## 2. Phubbing (Phone Snubbing)

- **Roberts, J.A., & David, M.E. (2016).** "My Life Has Become a Major Distraction from My Cell Phone: Partner Phubbing and Relationship Satisfaction." *Computers in Human Behavior*, 54, 134-141.

Partner phubbing (attending to your phone while with your partner) was significantly associated with:
- Reduced relationship satisfaction
- Increased conflict
- Depression in the phubbed partner

**Key finding:** Phubbing is strongly habitual — people often phub without awareness (Chotpitayasunondh & Douglas, 2016).

## 3. Turkle — "Alone Together" and Reclaiming Conversation

- **Turkle, S. (2011).** *Alone Together: Why We Expect More from Technology and Less from Each Other*. Basic Books.
- **Turkle, S. (2015).** *Reclaiming Conversation: The Power of Talk in a Digital Age*. Penguin Press.

**Key Thesis:** Digital technology creates the *illusion* of connection — we are perpetually connected but never fully present. We are "alone together."

**Specific Research Findings:**
- Young adults raised on heavy texting show measurable deficits in empathy compared to previous generations (Konrath et al., 2011 meta-analysis)
- Face-to-face conversation is being replaced by text, but text lacks the non-verbal nuance that carries most emotional communication
- People become less willing to tolerate the ambiguity and discomfort that genuine connection requires

## 4. Notification Design and Attention

- **Mark, G., Iqbal, S.T., Czerwinski, M., & Johns, P. (2015).** CSCW 2015. Interruptions (notifications) take an average of **23 minutes** to fully recover from. Real-time notifications fragment attention in ways that have compounding effects.

- **Williams et al. (2022).** Batching social notifications (rather than real-time delivery) produced equivalent responsiveness but significantly lower stress and better conversation quality during in-person time.

- **Kushlev, K., & Dunn, E.W. (2015).** "Checking Email Less Frequently Reduces Stress." *Computers in Human Behavior*, 43, 220-228. Reducing check-in frequency reduced stress without reducing communication effectiveness.

## 5. Digital Minimalism and Relationship Quality

- **Newport, C. (2019).** *Digital Minimalism: Choosing a Focused Life in a Noisy World*. Portfolio/Penguin.

Intentional, selective technology use improves relationship quality:
- High-quality relationship time requires uninterrupted presence — digital minimalism creates this
- Social media "likes" are "digital communication junk food" — low-calorie interaction that satisfies momentarily but doesn't nourish

---

## 6. Design Implications for a Personal CRM

The research converges on a clear design philosophy:

**An intentional CRM should:**
- Be used in scheduled, deliberate sessions (morning review, weekly planning) — not as a reactive notification stream
- Prompt for quality interactions (deeper conversations, higher-modality contact) — not just frequency
- Feel like a tool for genuine caring — the anti-social-media
- Provide calm, considered prompts rather than anxious, gamified nudges

**Batched review vs. real-time notifications:**
- A weekly digest of "who to reach out to this week" supports intentional relationship thinking
- Real-time push notifications for every reminder fragment attention and reduce quality
- Pre-contact briefs (what to know before reaching out to someone) support better conversations

# Keeping in Touch — Scientific Research

## 1. Weak Tie Theory (Granovetter)

Mark Granovetter's landmark 1973 paper *"The Strength of Weak Ties"* introduced a counterintuitive finding.

### Key Findings

- **Weak ties** (acquaintances you contact infrequently) are more valuable than strong ties for new information, job opportunities, and social mobility
- Strong ties cluster together — they mostly share the same information network
- Weak ties bridge different social clusters, providing access to diverse resources and perspectives
- Granovetter (2022, updated research) confirmed these findings persist in digital social networks

### Practical Implications

- Don't abandon casual acquaintances — systematically nurturing weak ties has outsized career and social value
- A CRM can help maintain these ties by prompting periodic contact even with people you don't think about daily

## 2. The Psychology of Reaching Out (Liu et al.)

Peggy Liu, Kate Townsend, and colleagues (2019, 2021) conducted multiple studies on the "undervalued maintenance of social connections."

### Key Findings

- People dramatically **underestimate how much others appreciate being reached out to** (especially out of the blue)
- Senders worried about seeming awkward or intrusive — but receivers reported feeling meaningfully positive
- "Thinking of you" messages had similar warmth impact to more elaborate contact
- The gap was larger for weak ties (we especially underestimate the impact on acquaintances)
- Even brief, simple outreach ("I saw this and thought of you") creates strong positive feelings in the recipient

### Practical Implications

- Build in prompts to reach out to people — even simple, low-effort contact is more valuable than not reaching out
- Track interests and circumstances to enable personalized "thinking of you" moments
- Overcome the false belief that you need a "good reason" to contact someone

## 3. Optimal Contact Frequency & Dunbar's Number

### Contact Time Required for Friendship (Hall, 2019)

- Casual friends: ~50 hours together
- Good friends: ~90 hours
- Close friends: ~200+ hours
- Without deliberate maintenance, relationships decay predictably over time

### Dunbar's Number (Dunbar, 1993, 2021)

Robin Dunbar's research identifies cognitive limits on relationship maintenance:

| Tier | Size | Contact Frequency |
|------|------|-------------------|
| Inner circle | ~5 | Weekly+ |
| Support clique | ~15 | Monthly |
| Sympathy group | ~50 | Quarterly |
| Dunbar's number | ~150 | 1-2x per year |
| Acquaintances | ~500 | Occasionally |

### Practical Implications

- Design reminder systems that tier contacts and prompt appropriate contact frequencies
- Allow users to set relationship tiers and automate/suggest check-in cadences
- Relationships at each tier require specific minimum contact frequencies to maintain their tier status

## 4. Memory and Context in Relationships

### Key Research

- **The "Knowing Them Well" Effect**: Research shows that people who demonstrate they remember personal details are perceived as more caring and attentive (Reis & Shaver, 1988, intimacy theory)
- **Personalization**: Simply referencing something specific to the person makes contact feel significantly more meaningful than generic outreach
- **Autobiographical Memory and Social Bonding**: Pillemer (2001) — specific, shared memories are particularly powerful for reconnecting relationships
- **Note-taking on People**: Systematic notes about people dramatically improve relationship quality and outcomes (Peppers & Rogers, 1993, CRM research)

### Practical Implications

- Every detail captured about a person (interests, circumstances, what they mentioned last time) becomes a future relationship asset
- Following up on things people mentioned previously ("How did that job interview go?") is one of the highest-impact relationship behaviors
- Notes and context stored in a CRM directly enable this behavior

## 5. Digital vs. In-Person Contact

### Research (Vlahovic, Roberts & Dunbar, 2012)

Contact modality ranked by effectiveness for relationship maintenance:

1. **Face-to-face** — Strongest endorphin release, most effective
2. **Video call** — Significantly better than phone/text for intimacy
3. **Phone call** — Better than text for relationship maintenance
4. **Text/email** — Lowest impact but better than nothing

### Other Findings

- High-quality video contact can partially substitute for in-person contact (Ballard et al., 2020)
- Social media contact alone is insufficient to maintain relationship closeness (Pollet, Roberts & Dunbar, 2011)

### Practical Implications

- Prompt higher-quality interactions for important relationships
- Track the modality of last contact — "last texted" vs. "last called" vs. "last saw in person" is meaningful

## 6. Relationship Rituals

### Research

- Gottman's research on rituals of connection: Shared rituals provide relationship glue that maintains closeness even across long gaps
- **Habit formation**: Implementation intentions ("every Sunday I will message one old friend") dramatically increase follow-through (Clear, 2018, *Atomic Habits*)
- Social routines research (Fiese et al., 2002): Consistent rituals correlate with higher satisfaction, security, and sense of belonging — extends from families to friendships

### Practical Implications

- Allow users to set recurring check-in reminders per person or group
- Gamification of consistency (streaks, patterns) can reinforce relationship rituals
- Celebrate relationship milestones (1 year of regular contact, etc.)

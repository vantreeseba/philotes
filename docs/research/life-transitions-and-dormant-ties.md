# Life Transitions, Dormant Ties & Network Change — Scientific Research

## 1. How Major Transitions Disrupt Social Networks

- **Bhattacharya, K., Ghosh, A., Monsivais, D., Dunbar, R.I.M., & Kaski, K. (2016).** "Sex Differences in Social Focus Across the Life Course in Humans." *Royal Society Open Science*, 3(4), 160097.

Using mobile phone data from 3.2 million users, this study tracked how networks reorganize around major life transitions.

**Key Disruptions and Their Effects:**

| Transition | Typical Network Effect |
|------------|------------------------|
| Starting university | Lose many old friends; gain new ones; core network disrupted |
| Entering a romantic relationship | Lose ~2 close friends (absorbed into couple focus) |
| Having children | Dramatic reduction in social contact; network shrinks significantly |
| Geographic relocation | ~50% of close relationships fade within 3 months without intentional maintenance |
| Retirement | Loss of work-based contacts; serious isolation risk without proactive rebuilding |

- **Dunbar, R.I.M. (2018).** "The Anatomy of Friendship." *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 22(1), 32-51. Networks peak in size during the 20s and decline progressively through midlife, stabilizing in the 50s–60s but with higher average quality.

**Key Insight:** Transitions are *predictable* network disruption events. A CRM that notes when someone (or you) has recently moved, changed jobs, had a child, or retired can prompt the right maintenance behaviors at the right time.

## 2. Dormant Ties — The Science of Reconnection

- **Levin, D.Z., Walter, J., & Murnighan, J.K. (2011).** "Dormant Ties: The Value of Reconnecting." *Organization Science*, 22(4), 923-939.

"Dormant ties" are relationships that were once active but have become inactive — people you used to know well but have lost regular contact with.

**Key Findings:**
- Dormant ties are **unexpectedly rich sources of information and help** compared to current active ties
- Reconnected dormant ties provided information perceived as *more novel* and *more useful* than current active ties
- **Why:** Dormant ties retain trust from the original relationship but have developed divergent experiences and networks since — providing new information without having to build trust from scratch
- Reconnecting is typically *easier* than building a new relationship

**What This Means for a CRM:**
Dormant ties (people not contacted in 1–2+ years, who were previously active) have disproportionate potential value and are often neglected precisely because they're no longer top-of-mind.

**Practical Implications:**
- A "dormant ties" view — people you haven't contacted in over a year who were previously frequent — could be a high-ROI feature
- Reconnection messages can reference the original relationship directly ("I was thinking about when we...") — this is warmly received and comfortable
- The barrier to reconnection is usually initiator anxiety, not recipient receptiveness (Liu et al., 2023)

## 3. Building/Rebuilding Networks After Moves

- **Hampton, K., & Wellman, B. (2003).** "Neighboring in Netville." *City & Community*, 2(4), 277-311. Research on network-building after geographic moves shows people who proactively used digital tools to maintain old ties *and* build new ones had significantly better social outcomes 1 year post-move.

**After a major move, two things must happen simultaneously:**
1. Maintain old relationships deliberately — they will not maintain themselves
2. Accelerate new relationship formation — requires intentional investment

## 4. Socioemotional Selectivity Theory — Aging and Networks

- **Carstensen, L.L. (1995).** "Evidence for a Life-Span Theory of Socioemotional Selectivity." *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 4(5), 151-156.

As people age and perceive time as more limited, they **selectively reduce their social networks to the most meaningful relationships**. This is *adaptive*, not a symptom of decline:
- Older adults have fewer but more satisfying and closer relationships
- They invest more selectively in relationships with high emotional payoff
- They are better at regulating negative emotions in social contexts

**Lifespan Network Patterns:**

| Life Stage | Network Pattern |
|------------|----------------|
| Teens/20s | Expanding, exploratory, high weak-tie activity |
| 30s | Contraction (career, family), selective deepening |
| 40s–50s | Stable core network; intentional maintenance required |
| 60s+ | Further contraction; focus on highest-quality relationships |

**Midlife Friendship Research:**
- **Blieszner, R. (2014).** "The Worth of Friendship." *Generations*, 38(1), 24-30. Midlife friendships require *more* intentional maintenance than those formed in youth — the automatic structural context (school, neighborhood) has disappeared, and the relationship depends entirely on deliberate investment.

## 5. Practical Implications for a Personal CRM

- **Transition tracking per person**: note when someone has moved, changed jobs, had a child, retired — these are high-attention windows
- **Dormant tie detection**: surface contacts not reached in 12+ months who were previously frequent
- **"Reconnect" prompts** with context: "You haven't spoken to [person] in 18 months — you were close in 2023"
- **Life stage awareness**: older users may need different defaults (fewer, deeper contacts); younger users may benefit from weak-tie maintenance prompts

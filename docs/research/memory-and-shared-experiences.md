# Emotional Memory, Shared Experiences & Meaning-Making in Relationships

## 1. Shared Storytelling and "We-Narratives"

- **Pasupathi, M., & Hoyt, T. (2009).** "The Development of Narrative Identity in Late Adolescence and Emergent Adulthood." *Developmental Psychology*, 45(2), 558-574.

Research on collaborative narratives shows that how two people co-construct and tell shared stories shapes the strength and quality of their relationship:

**Key Findings:**
- **"We-stories"** — narratives told jointly about shared experiences — activate feelings of shared identity and commitment
- How a listener *responds* to a shared story (confirming, elaborating, or contradicting) shapes how the event is encoded in both memories
- Couples and close friends who can jointly narrate their shared history feel more bonded than those with parallel but separate accounts of the same events

## 2. Joint Reminiscing as a Bonding Mechanism

- **Fivush, R., & Zaman, W. (2015).** "Disentangling Motivations: Gender, Power, and Narrative in Reminiscing." *Memory*, 23(1), 55-66.

*Joint reminiscing* — recalling shared experiences together — reinforces relationship bonds more effectively than individual remembering. When two people return to a shared memory together, they co-regulate the emotional meaning of that experience.

**The Nostalgia Effect:**

- **Wildschut, T., Sedikides, C., Arndt, J., & Routledge, C. (2006).** "Nostalgia: Content, Triggers, Functions." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 91(5), 975-993.

Nostalgia is primarily a *social* emotion — nostalgic memories almost always involve close others. Inducing nostalgia:
- Increases social connectedness
- Increases feelings of closeness
- Increases willingness to give social support
- Counteracts loneliness

**Practical CRM Implication:** Prompting someone to reminisce about a positive shared experience *before* reaching out to someone they haven't spoken to in a while could powerfully motivate contact and improve the quality of the subsequent conversation.

## 3. Peak Experiences as Relationship Anchors

- **Brewer, W.F. (1988).** "Memory for randomly sampled autobiographical events." In *Remembering Reconsidered.* Research shows that highly emotional, distinctive events are remembered far better than routine events.

- Shared peak experiences (a challenging trip together, a difficult shared period, a joyful celebration) become **"anchor memories"** that define the relationship — the events people will still talk about decades later.

**Key Insight:**
The strength of a relationship is partly encoded in its *library* of shared peak experiences. Adding new shared experiences deepens bonds in ways that pure maintenance (check-ins, logistics) cannot.

**Practical Implications:**
- Capturing significant shared memories and experiences in a CRM creates a record of the relationship's "anchor points"
- "On this date two years ago you and [person] did X..." prompts could be powerful reconnection triggers
- Tracking shared experiences (trips, events, milestones) gives a qualitatively richer relationship record than just contact dates

## 4. The "Cognitive Savings" of Notes on People

- Human episodic memory follows the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve: ~50% lost within an hour, ~70% within a day, without reinforcement
- External memory systems (Clark & Chalmers, 1998, "extended mind" theory) genuinely extend functional memory
- Writing about significant relationship events processes emotions, improves clarity, and creates psychological distance that improves decision-making (Pennebaker, 1997)

**What to Record:**
Notes about a person's current challenges, their big news, what they said last time, upcoming events they mentioned — all become high-value inputs for future interactions. Reviewing them before contact transforms generic outreach into felt, personal care.

## 5. Practical Implications for a Personal CRM

- A "shared memories" section per person (not just notes, but specifically flagged "significant experiences we've had together")
- "On this date" prompts surfacing anniversaries of shared experiences
- Pre-contact view that surfaces positive shared memories to motivate and warm up a reach-out
- Annual relationship reflections: "Looking back at your year with [person]..."

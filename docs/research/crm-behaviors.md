# Practical CRM & Relationship Management Behaviors — Research

## 1. Value of Notes and Memory Systems

### Research Basis

- **Cognitive offloading (Risko & Gilbert, 2016)**: External memory systems (notes, lists) reliably improve recall and reduce cognitive load — allowing more attention to the person during interactions
- CRM adoption research (Rigby, Reichheld & Schefter, 2002; Salesforce research): Systematic tracking of relationship context dramatically improves outcomes in both commercial and personal relationship contexts
- **Contextual cuing**: Memory research shows that storing context cues (what someone was worried about, what they're excited about) makes recall dramatically more reliable

### Practical Implications

- Make note-taking fast and frictionless — every second of friction reduces use
- Notes should be searchable, linked to people, and surfaced at the right moments
- Notes should be able to surface relevant context when prompting contact ("You haven't contacted John in 3 months — he was job hunting last time you spoke")

## 2. Personalization in Outreach

### Research

- **Personalization Effect**: Research consistently shows that personalized messages are dramatically more effective than generic ones — even minor personalization (using someone's name) improves response rates and positive affect
- **Interest Matching (Chen et al., 2010)**: Matching communication content to known interests of the recipient increased engagement and relationship warmth
- **The Callback Effect**: Referencing something from a previous interaction is one of the highest-impact relationship maintenance behaviors — it demonstrates listening, memory, and care

### Practical Implications

- Track interests, hobbies, circumstances, and past conversation topics per person
- Surface relevant context when prompting users to reach out
- Enable structured notes beyond free-form: tags, categories, follow-up items

---

## 3. Birthdays, Anniversaries & Milestones

### Research

- Birthday acknowledgment leverages multiple well-documented mechanisms: surprise outreach, personalization, feeling known and remembered
- **Temporal Landmarks (Dai et al., 2014)**: People are more receptive to fresh-start thinking around significant dates (birthdays, New Year's, anniversaries)
- **Milestone recognition (Amabile & Kramer, 2011)**: Acknowledging progress and milestones significantly boosts positive emotion and relationship quality
- Anniversaries of shared experiences are powerful reconnection triggers

### Practical Implications

- Build a robust "upcoming important dates" view with advance notice (not just on the day)
- Track personal milestones beyond birthdays: job changes, moves, graduations, health events
- Surface dates in advance to enable thoughtful, personalized responses

---

## 4. Relationship Rituals and Habit Formation

### Research

- Gottman: Shared rituals provide relationship glue that maintains closeness even across long gaps
- **Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer, 1999)**: Forming specific plans ("I will do X at time Y in situation Z") dramatically increases follow-through vs. general intentions
- **Habit stacking (Clear, 2018)**: Attaching new habits to existing ones improves consistency
- **Commitment devices**: Pre-committing to regular contact (scheduling it) increases actual follow-through

### Practical Implications

- Allow users to set recurring check-in reminders per person
- Suggest cadences based on relationship tier (Dunbar model)
- Gamification of consistency can reinforce habits without feeling manipulative

## 5. Tiering and Prioritizing Relationships

### Research (Dunbar, 1993, 2021)

Dunbar's social brain hypothesis implies that humans have a finite cognitive budget for relationships. Optimal allocation:

| Tier           | Approx. Size | Investment Level | Suggested Contact |
| -------------- | ------------ | ---------------- | ----------------- |
| Inner circle   | 1–5          | Very high        | Weekly+           |
| Close friends  | 6–15         | High             | Monthly           |
| Good friends   | 16–50        | Medium           | Quarterly         |
| Friends        | 51–150       | Low              | 1-2x/year         |
| Acquaintances  | 151–500      | Minimal          | Occasionally      |

### Practical Implications

- Provide a relationship tier/priority field per person
- Filter and sort contact lists by tier to focus attention
- Use tiers to drive intelligent reminder defaults

## 6. Contact Logging and Recency

### Research

- The absence of contact is often the first sign of relationship decay (Christakis & Fowler, 2009)
- Research on friendship dissolution shows that relationships decay predictably when contact drops below maintenance thresholds
- Logging contact creates accountability and awareness of neglected relationships

### Practical Implications

- Log contact events with modality (in-person, call, text) and date
- Surface "last contacted" prominently in the UI
- Flag relationships that have exceeded their tier's recommended contact interval
- Enable users to log what was discussed — not just that contact occurred

---

## 7. Key Design Principles for a Personal CRM

Based on the research, the most impactful features of a personal CRM are:

1. **Friction-free note capture** — Users won't take notes if it's hard
2. **Upcoming dates dashboard** — Birthdays, anniversaries, milestones in one view
3. **Contact recency and reminders** — Who have you not talked to recently?
4. **Contextual prompting** — Surface relevant notes when it's time to reach out
5. **Relationship tiers** — Help users allocate attention where it matters most
6. **Follow-up tracking** — Items from past conversations that deserve follow-up
7. **Gratitude prompts** — Nudge users to express gratitude deliberately

# Prosocial Behavior Change & Motivation — Scientific Research

## 1. Implementation Intentions Applied to Social Goals

- **Gollwitzer, P.M., & Sheeran, P. (2006).** "Implementation Intentions and Goal Achievement: A Meta-Analysis of Effects and Processes." *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*, 38, 69-119.

Meta-analysis of 94 studies (N = 8,461): implementation intentions — specific "if-then" plans — increased goal achievement by a medium-to-large effect size (d = .65). Particularly effective for goals requiring:
- Overcoming habits and competing demands
- Initiating behaviors despite inertia or anxiety

**Applied to Relationship Maintenance:**
- "If it is Sunday evening, then I will review my contacts and reach out to one person I haven't spoken to in 3 months"
- "If I hear that [person] is going through [situation], then I will call rather than text"
- Prompting users to form *specific plans* (not just intentions) dramatically increases follow-through

---

## 2. Temptation Bundling (Milkman)

- **Milkman, K.L., Minson, J.A., & Volpp, K.G. (2014).** "Holding the Hunger Games Hostage at the Gym: An Evaluation of Temptation Bundling." *Management Science*, 60(2), 283-299.

"Temptation bundling" = pairing an enjoyable activity with an effortful one. Participants who could only access a favorite audiobook while exercising exercised significantly more.

**Applied to a Personal CRM:**
- Bundle relationship review with a favorite morning ritual (coffee, music)
- Make the CRM experience itself pleasant — beautiful, calm, rewarding — so reviewing relationships feels like a treat, not a chore
- UX quality directly affects usage frequency for this type of tool

## 3. When Gamification Helps vs. Hurts

### The Overjustification Effect

- **Deci, E.L., Koestner, R., & Ryan, R.M. (1999).** Meta-analysis (128 studies): Tangible rewards contingent on task engagement *reliably undermine* intrinsic motivation over time.

- **Przybylski, A.K., Rigby, C.S., & Ryan, R.M. (2010).** "A Motivational Model of Video Game Engagement." *Review of General Psychology*, 14(2), 154-166.

Gamification supports intrinsic motivation *only* when:
- Feedback feels **informative** (not controlling)
- The person feels the system helps them do something they *already want* to do
- Rewards are not contingent on task engagement

**What to Avoid:**
- Streaks and leaderboards → likely to undermine long-term intrinsic motivation
- Controlling prompts ("You haven't done X, you should") → feel obligating, not enabling
- Competitive mechanics → relationship maintenance is not competitive

**What Works:**
- Informational feedback: "You've connected with all your close friends this month" → supportive
- Autonomy-supporting prompts: "Here's a suggestion — you decide" → preserves agency
- Progress reflection (not score): "Here's how your relationships looked this year" → meaningful

## 4. Self-Determination Theory — Relatedness as a Basic Need

- **Deci, E.L., & Ryan, R.M. (2000).** "The 'What' and 'Why' of Goal Pursuits: Human Needs and the Self-Determination of Behavior." *Psychological Inquiry*, 11(4), 227-268. [50,000+ citations]

SDT identifies three basic psychological needs:
1. **Autonomy** — feeling choice and volition
2. **Competence** — feeling effective and capable
3. **Relatedness** — feeling connected to and cared for by others

**Relatedness is non-optional** — its deprivation (loneliness) causes harm even when autonomy and competence needs are met. Tools that support relationship behaviors must do so in ways that feel autonomy-preserving, not obligating.

## 5. Habit Formation for Social Behaviors

- **Lally, P., van Jaarsveld, C.H.M., Potts, H.W.W., & Wardle, J. (2010).** "How Are Habits Formed: Modelling Habit Formation in the Real World." *European Journal of Social Psychology*, 40(6), 998-1009.

Average habit formation time: **66 days** (range: 18–254 days). Simple, consistent behaviors form faster. Context stability is essential — habits tied to specific contexts persist better through disruption.

**For a CRM:**
- Help users establish a stable weekly review routine above all else
- Early prompts should be lower-friction; complexity can be added once the routine is established
- The same time and context (Sunday evening, morning coffee) helps the habit form faster

- **Wood, W., & Neal, D.T. (2007).** "A New Look at Habits and the Habit-Goal Interface." *Psychological Review*, 114(4), 843-863. The cue-routine-reward loop for social habits:
  - **Cue**: Open app → weekly review
  - **Routine**: Review contacts, send one message
  - **Reward**: Feeling of genuine care expressed; response received

## 6. Commitment Devices

- **Bryan, G., Karlan, D., & Nelson, S. (2010).** "Commitment Devices." *Annual Review of Economics*, 2, 671-698. Pre-committing to a behavior increases follow-through, especially for behaviors requiring overcoming present-bias.

**For relationships:**
- Scheduling a call and adding it to a calendar is a commitment device
- Recurring reminders set at the start of a relationship cadence create a commitment structure
- Telling a mutual friend "I'm going to reach out to X this week" creates social accountability

---

## 7. The "Energy Giver" Concept

- **Cross, R., Baker, W., & Parker, A. (2003).** "What Creates Energy in Organizations." *MIT Sloan Management Review*, 44(4), 51-57.

Some people consistently leave others feeling energized; others consistently deplete energy. "Energy givers" are disproportionately sought out, trusted, and influential — independent of formal role or expertise.

**Energizing behaviors (learnable, not just innate):**
- Showing genuine curiosity about the other person
- Being fully present (not distracted)
- Generating possibilities vs. focusing on problems
- Celebrating others' wins enthusiastically (Active Constructive Responding)

**The Heliotropic Effect:** People unconsciously orient toward those who energize them and away from those who deplete them. Being an "energy giver" is a relationship superpower.

---

## 8. Practical Implications for a Personal CRM

- **Weekly review session design** — not a notifications dashboard, but a calm weekly ritual
- **Implementation intention prompts** — help users form specific plans, not just set intentions
- **Informational (not controlling) feedback** — "Here's a reflection of your social year" not "You're behind on your goals"
- **Beautiful, intentional UX** — the experience itself should feel like caring, not task management
- **Habit formation support** — same time/day prompts; celebrate 66-day consistency milestones
